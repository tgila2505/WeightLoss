## What intermittent fasting actually is

Intermittent fasting (IF) is an eating *pattern*, not a diet. Instead of restricting what you eat, you restrict **when** you eat.

The most popular protocols:

- **16:8** — fast for 16 hours, eat within an 8-hour window (e.g. 12pm–8pm)
- **5:2** — eat normally 5 days, restrict to ~500 kcal on 2 non-consecutive days
- **OMAD** — one meal a day (extreme; not recommended for most people)

The appeal is obvious: no calorie counting, no food rules, just a time constraint.

## What the research actually shows

Here's the honest summary of the science:

> **Intermittent fasting works because it helps people eat fewer calories — not because of any metabolic magic.**

A 2020 meta-analysis in *Obesity Reviews* comparing IF to continuous calorie restriction found **no significant difference** in weight loss, fat loss, or muscle retention when total calories were matched.

IF is a tool to achieve a calorie deficit, not a superior metabolic state.

That said, it works **very well** for a large group of people because:

1. Skipping breakfast feels easier than tracking every meal
2. A compressed eating window naturally limits snacking
3. It removes decision fatigue around food

## Who benefits most from IF

IF tends to work best for people who:

- Often overeat in the evenings (the 12pm–8pm window helps here)
- Dislike tracking calories meticulously
- Have stable energy without breakfast
- Can tolerate hunger in the morning

It tends to work **poorly** for people who:

- Wake up ravenous
- Do intense morning exercise
- Have a history of disordered eating
- Work jobs with irregular or social lunch schedules

## The muscle-loss concern

A common worry: does fasting burn muscle?

The short answer is **no, as long as you hit your protein targets** within your eating window. Research shows no meaningful difference in lean mass retention between IF and standard dieting when protein intake is adequate (1.6–2.2 g/kg body weight).

Practical tip: front-load your protein. Aim for **at least 40 g of protein** in your first meal to trigger muscle protein synthesis early.

## Practical 16:8 setup

If you want to try 16:8, here's a sensible starting point:

1. **Skip breakfast** — black coffee, tea, or water only until noon
2. **First meal at 12pm** — high-protein, moderate carb: eggs, Greek yoghurt, grilled chicken
3. **Second meal at 4–5pm** — balanced main: protein + veg + complex carb
4. **Optional snack at 7pm** — if needed to hit protein/calorie targets
5. **Stop eating at 8pm**

You don't need to be exact. A 14-hour fast on some days and 17 hours on others is fine — the consistency of the *habit* matters more than the precise window.

## The real reason most IF attempts fail

People treat the eating window as a licence to eat anything. The calories still count.

If you're eating 3,500 kcal in your 8-hour window, IF won't create the deficit needed for fat loss. The window is a constraint on *when* — you still need to eat the right *amount*.

## Should you try it?

If the idea of skipping breakfast feels natural (many people report they weren't very hungry in the morning anyway), IF is an excellent low-friction approach to maintaining a calorie deficit.

If you love breakfast or feel terrible without it, skip IF entirely and use simple portion tracking instead. Neither method is objectively superior — the best diet is the one you'll actually stick to.
