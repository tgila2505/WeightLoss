## The one-number that controls your weight

Everything you've heard about carbs, detoxes, and "superfoods" is noise. The mechanism that determines whether you lose, gain, or maintain weight is brutally simple: **calories in vs calories out**.

Eat fewer calories than your body burns and you lose weight. Eat more and you gain. No exception.

The question isn't *whether* this is true — it's *how many calories* should you actually eat.

## Step 1: Find your TDEE

Your **Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE)** is the number of calories your body burns each day, including your baseline metabolism and your activity level.

A simple estimate for most adults:

- **Sedentary** (desk job, little exercise): body weight in kg × 28–31
- **Lightly active** (3 workouts/week): body weight in kg × 32–34
- **Moderately active** (5 workouts/week): body weight in kg × 35–38

So a 75 kg person with a desk job has a TDEE of roughly **2,100–2,325 kcal/day**.

## Step 2: Set the right deficit

A **500 kcal/day deficit** produces approximately 0.5 kg of fat loss per week. This is the most widely recommended starting point because it's:

- Fast enough to see meaningful progress
- Small enough to preserve muscle and avoid metabolic adaptation
- Sustainable for months without burnout

> **Avoid going below 1,200 kcal/day** (women) or **1,500 kcal/day** (men). Severe restriction triggers muscle loss, metabolic slowdown, and rebound eating.

## Step 3: Protect your muscle with protein

When you're in a deficit, your body will burn fat *and* muscle unless you give it a reason to keep the muscle. That reason is protein.

Target **1.6–2.2 g of protein per kg of body weight** daily. For our 75 kg example: **120–165 g of protein per day**.

High-protein sources:
- Chicken breast (31 g per 100 g)
- Greek yoghurt (10 g per 100 g)
- Eggs (6 g per egg)
- Lentils (9 g per 100 g cooked)
- Cottage cheese (12 g per 100 g)

## The metabolism-crash myth (and the truth)

You may have heard that prolonged dieting "breaks" your metabolism. Here's what actually happens:

As you lose weight, your **TDEE decreases** — partly because you weigh less, and partly because your body adapts slightly to the deficit. This is called **metabolic adaptation**.

The fix is simple: **recalculate your TDEE every 4–6 weeks** as your weight drops. Our app does this automatically with each weigh-in.

## How to track without losing your mind

Tracking calories is effective but can feel tedious. Here's a minimal approach:

1. **Weigh yourself daily** (use a 7-day rolling average)
2. **Log your biggest meals** — breakfast, lunch, dinner — and estimate snacks
3. **Batch-cook proteins** so you're not logging every ingredient

After 2–3 weeks, most people develop an intuitive sense of portion sizes and can track mentally.

## The bottom line

1. Calculate your TDEE
2. Subtract 400–600 kcal/day
3. Hit your protein target
4. Adjust every 4 weeks based on results

Weight loss is a maths problem. Get the maths right and the results follow.
